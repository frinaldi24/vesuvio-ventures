## 1. Thesis

*Europe is committing to rearm and to build sovereign defence capacity for the first time in a generation; if a Palantir-alumni team can build the hardware-software-manufacturing backbone for that effort, they are early to one of the largest, most durable spending waves in Europe — and one where "European-owned" is a hard requirement, not a preference.*

This is the opportunistic sleeve's macro bet. The investment case rests less on a product (there isn't one yet) and more on a rare alignment: an exceptional, mission-credentialed team, a generational shift in European defence spending, and a structural reason a *European* champion must exist because sovereignty rules out the American incumbents for many contracts. High risk, but the kind of asymmetry the sleeve exists to capture.

## 2. What they do

Onodrim is building the physical and digital "plumbing" of European defence: **manufacturing capacity, multi-domain sensors, and networked, interoperable systems** spanning sensing, production, and data. Stated priorities are border protection and resilient supply chains for mission-critical systems — defending Europe's "eastern flank." It is explicitly positioned as a **sovereign European alternative to Palantir + Anduril**.

- **Target customer:** European governments and militaries, allied forces, and defence primes.
- **Stage:** very early. Just out of stealth, **no public product, revenue, or contracts** — this is a team-and-thesis bet, with capital going to R&D, hiring, and manufacturing build-out.

## 3. Market

- Europe is expected to mobilise on the order of **€800B in defence spending by 2030** (founder framing), driven by the strategic-autonomy push after Ukraine.
- Q1 2026 European tech funding was strong (~€20.2B), with unusual appetite for defence tech — Onodrim was the **#2 largest European seed of Q1 2026**, behind only France's ~$1B AMI round.
- **Who pays:** European states and primes — buyers with deep pockets and, increasingly, a mandate to buy European. That sovereignty mandate is the structural moat the incumbents can't easily cross.

## 4. Team

- **Aistis Šimaitis (co-founder, CEO)** — ex-Palantir technical leader, former Lithuanian government advisor, Oxford DPhil. Mission-credentialed and from a front-line state.
- **Alexander Blessing (co-founder, CTO)** — ex-Palantir engineering leader; Stanford + Cambridge.
- **Christian Garrett (co-founder)** — Italian-American; also a partner at VC 201 Ventures, which is on the cap table — an investor-operator dual role that warrants governance diligence.

Seed **co-led by Founders Fund, Lakestar, and General Catalyst**, with Iron Wolf Capital, Air Street, and others — a top-tier, transatlantic, defence-literate syndicate. The quality of backers for a pre-product seed is itself a strong signal.

**Price discipline note.** A €40M pre-product seed at an estimated ~€150M post is **richly priced** — this is momentum pricing on team and macro, exactly the kind of round the anti-portfolio warns about. I take a **smaller position** and am explicit about the tension: I'm paying up for a team-and-thesis bet with no product. The justification is the asymmetry (a European defence champion is a multi-billion outcome) and the sovereignty moat — but this position must be watched hardest for execution, and reserves (1x) are contingent on real contract wins, not more announcements.

## 6. Risks (pre-mortem)

1. **Execution against an enormous scope (primary risk).** Pre-product, no revenue, and a hardware + software + manufacturing ambition that is extraordinarily capital-intensive. €40M is large for a seed but tiny against that scope; the plan may simply be too broad for an early team.
2. **Procurement reality.** European defence procurement is slow, fragmented along national lines, incumbent-driven, and politically exposed. Time-to-revenue could be measured in years.
3. **Formidable competition.** Palantir and Anduril are far better capitalised and already winning European contracts; well-funded natives like Helsing are ahead. Onodrim must convert the sovereignty argument into actual wins.
4. **Capital intensity & dilution.** This will need very large follow-on rounds; early minority holders risk heavy dilution unless they can keep funding.
5. **Governance.** The co-founder/investor dual role (201 Ventures) needs clean diligence.

## 7. The bet & the plan

**Conviction: thesis-high, execution-unproven.** I believe the macro (European rearmament + sovereignty mandate) is real and durable, and the team is genuinely exceptional for this mission. What's entirely unproven is whether they can build and *sell* into one of the hardest customer bases in the world. This is a small, high-asymmetry position, not a core holding.

**What I do in the 18 months after wiring:**
- **Track exactly one thing above all: the first real contract** (or credible government/prime pilot). In defence, a signed customer is worth more than any technology demo.
- **Watch capital efficiency and scope discipline** — is the team focusing on a winnable beachhead, or spreading thin across the whole "plumbing" vision?
- **Resolve governance** on the investor-operator co-founder before any follow-on.
- **Hold 1x reserves for the Series A, conditional on contract traction**, and be realistic that maintaining ownership through capital-intensive later rounds may not be feasible for a €50M fund — this may be a position we ride rather than defend.

**Kill criteria:** 18–24 months post-seed with no meaningful contract or funded pilot, and evidence of unfocused scope/burn → no follow-on, mark down.
