## 1. Thesis

*A repeat founding team that already built a category leader is betting that enterprises don't want AI chatbots — they want custom software that quietly takes over back-office work — and if "AI product team as a service" becomes the default way large industrials automate, Pit is early to a very large market.*

The opportunistic sleeve is founder-led by design, and Pit is the archetype: three of Voi's four cofounders, plus alumni from Klarna and iZettle, backed by a16z on their first disclosed round. When a team that scaled a company to ~1,000 people across 13 countries starts something new and the best growth investors pre-empt it, that pattern is worth paying attention to — even before the product is proven.

## 2. What they do

An enterprise describes an internal workflow — validating invoices, checking contracts, executing marketing campaigns — and Pit **automatically builds custom software that takes over the manual process**. It is explicitly *not* a chatbot or copilot. Two products:

- **Pit Studio** — learns a workflow and builds the system that runs it.
- **Pit Cloud** — governed infrastructure to run it in production (ISO 27001, SSO, role-based access, audit logs).

Scope is deliberately **internal/back-office only** ("nothing customer-facing"), and the stack is vendor-agnostic with an EU-models-on-EU-compute sovereignty angle. Target customers are **large industrials** — logistics, telecom, e-commerce, healthcare, manufacturing. Go-to-market uses forward-deployed engineers.

## 3. Market

- Pit sits in the fast-growing AI-software-development category (an adjacent reference market, AI code tools, was ~$4.9B in 2023 with multi-x growth projected by 2030).
- The deeper tailwind is the shift from **chatbots to agentic AI that does work**, plus Europe's sovereign-tech push and its large industrial base — a real reason a Stockholm company can win European enterprise.
- **Who pays:** large enterprises replacing manual back-office labour. If Pit's per-deployment savings claims (10,000+ hours/year, 85% faster campaign execution) generalise, the value proposition is straightforward CFO math.

## 4. Team

- **Adam Jafer (CEO, founding engineer)** — Voi cofounder; ~7 years scaling Voi to ~1,000 employees across 13 countries.
- **Filip Lindvall** — Voi cofounder, engineering.
- Founding team drawn from **Voi, Klarna, iZettle** — a deep Stockholm operator bench.
- **Fredrik Hjelm** (Voi CEO) is a co-founder but expected to be less hands-on — a flag on attention, not a dealbreaker.

Seed led by **a16z** (GPs Alex Rampell and Gabriel Vasquez), with Lakestar and angels from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Deel and Revolut, plus the Stena and Lundin families. That is an unusually strong seed signal.

**Price discipline note.** A pre-empted a16z seed for a hot repeat team is *expensive for a seed* — an estimated ~$70M post before meaningful revenue prices in the pedigree. Our ~2.9% is below target and reflects both round competitiveness and price. I'm comfortable taking a smaller, priced position here *because it's the opportunistic sleeve and the founder signal is exceptional*, but I would not chase the price higher. Reserves (1x) are held for a Series A that must be justified by **real, non-founder-network customer traction**, not more logos.

## 6. Risks (pre-mortem)

1. **Crowded, well-funded category (primary risk).** Lovable, Cursor, Bolt, Claude Code broadly; Wonderful, Distyl, Edra as deployment peers. "Governed enterprise deployment" is a sensible wedge but not obviously a durable moat.
2. **Services-heavy margins.** The forward-deployed-engineer model can cap gross margins and make the "durable software product" thesis hard to scale — the difference between a great consultancy and a venture-scale software company.
3. **Traction is early and network-tied.** Flagship customers (Voi, Stena) are close to the founders; the proof will be arm's-length enterprise wins that renew and expand.
4. **Founder attention.** The Voi CEO's involvement is a question mark on focus.
5. **Model-provider dependency & reputational noise.** Reliance on third-party models, and some founder-marketing controversy, are minor but real.

## 7. The bet & the plan

**Conviction: medium-high on team, unproven on business model.** This is exactly what the opportunistic sleeve is for — an exceptional team early to a large shift, at a price that demands the team deliver. The upside is a category-defining European enterprise-AI company; the risk is a high-quality services business that never earns a software multiple.

**What I do in the 18 months after wiring:**
- **Watch the metric that matters: net revenue retention on arm's-length customers.** Logos are cheap; expansion inside non-network enterprises is the real signal.
- **Interrogate gross margin trajectory** — is each deployment getting cheaper to deliver (product) or staying labour-heavy (services)? This determines whether reserves get deployed.
- **Hold 1x reserves for the Series A**, releasing only on evidence of durable, expanding, non-founder-network revenue.
- **Leverage the a16z signal** for downstream introductions, but don't confuse a great cap table for product-market fit.

**Kill criteria:** at Series A, revenue is concentrated in founder-network accounts and gross margins remain services-like → no follow-on.
