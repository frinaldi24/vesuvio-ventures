## 1. Thesis

*If "designer recombinases" can precisely insert and rewrite large stretches of DNA where CRISPR cannot, Seamless owns a gene-editing platform that addresses diseases the current tools can't reach — and Eli Lilly's billion-dollar deal says a top acquirer already believes it.*

CRISPR is superb at *cutting* DNA but limited at making large, precise *insertions*, and it relies on the cell's error-prone repair machinery. Seamless is a bet that a different, programmable enzyme class — recombinases — can do what CRISPR can't, opening a distinct and defensible slice of genetic medicine. The bet was substantially de-risked in January 2026 when Eli Lilly signed a collaboration worth over $1.12B in potential payments.

## 2. What they do

Seamless engineers **"designer recombinases"** — programmable enzymes that can **insert, exchange, invert, or excise** DNA at a chosen site in the genome. The differentiators versus CRISPR:

- Makes **large, precise DNA insertions** (pre-clinical evidence of precisely inverting a 138-kilobase fragment).
- Works **independently of the cell's natural (error-prone) DNA repair pathway**.
- Functions in **non-dividing cells** and can address multiple mutations with a single therapeutic.
- Sidesteps the payload-size limits that constrain conventional AAV gene therapy.

**Stage:** pre-clinical / discovery. No proprietary clinical programs yet — the near-term value is the **platform** and its validation through partnerships.

## 3. Market

- **Gene editing / genetic medicine** is a multi-billion-dollar field with several approved products and heavy pharma investment.
- **Beachhead — genetic hearing loss:** a rapidly validating space. Regeneron's Otarmeni became the first FDA-approved gene therapy for genetic (OTOF) hearing loss; AstraZeneca/Akouos, Sensorion and others are advancing. Notably, **Lilly itself acquired Akouos ($487M, 2022)** and is now *also* partnering Seamless — a strong signal of conviction in both the target and the platform.
- **Who pays:** pharma, via platform licensing (already happening) and, longer term, acquisition. Seamless's model monetises the platform *before* it needs to fund its own clinical trials — capital-efficient for a small company.

## 4. Team

- **Prof. Frank Buchholz (scientific founder)** — Head of Medical Systems Biology at TU Dresden; his lab pioneered recombinase programming. The science originates with him.
- **Felix Lansing (co-founder, CSO)** — built the platform as Buchholz's PhD student.
- **Albert Seymour (President & CEO, from April 2024)** — former President & CEO of rare-disease biotech Homology Medicines; brings US drug-development and dealmaking experience.
- Backed by **Forbion** and **Wellington Partners** — two of Europe's most credible life-science investors — plus non-dilutive German government (GO-Bio) funding.

## 6. Risks (pre-mortem)

1. **Platform doesn't generalise (primary risk).** Impressive pre-clinical edits do not guarantee therapeutic-grade precision, efficiency, and safety across many targets in humans. Recombinase programmability at scale is unproven clinically.
2. **Delivery and immunogenicity.** Like all in-vivo genetic medicines, Seamless still faces the delivery problem (AAV/LNP) and potential immune responses — risks it shares with the whole field.
3. **The beachhead is already competitive.** OTOF hearing-loss gene therapy has an *approved* product (Regeneron) and multiple advanced players. Seamless must prove recombinases offer a real advantage (larger genes, non-dividing cells) over established AAV approaches.
4. **Partnership-dependence.** Near-term validation and non-dilutive cash lean heavily on the Lilly deal; milestone-based economics mean value accrues slowly and can stall if programs slip.
5. **Small balance sheet.** ~$25M raised; the company needs the Series A (and partners) to fund an ambitious platform.

## 7. The bet & the plan

**Conviction: high, and improved by the Lilly validation.** This is the most *asymmetric* position in the life-science sleeve: a modest seed entry into a genuinely differentiated platform, with a top-ten pharma already paying to access it. A platform that works becomes a serial deal-generator and a prime acquisition target.

**What I do in the 18 months after wiring:**
- **Use the Lilly deal as the scoreboard:** track progress and milestone triggers in that collaboration — it is the clearest external read on whether the platform delivers.
- **Deploy 2x reserves into the Series A** to defend and grow ownership, now that partnership validation has lowered platform risk.
- **Watch for a second pharma deal** — one partnership can be idiosyncratic; two would confirm the platform is broadly wanted and re-rate the company decisively.
- **Diligence the delivery strategy** — the recombinase is only as good as the vehicle that gets it into the right cells; this is where I'd want independent conviction before doubling down hard.

**Kill criteria:** Lilly collaboration stalls at the first milestone *and* no second partner emerges *and* platform data fails to generalise beyond the hearing-loss target → no Series A follow-on, mark down.
