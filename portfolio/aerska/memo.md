## 1. Thesis

*If Aerska's brain-shuttle can deliver RNAi durably and uniformly into the brain from a simple injection, it unlocks an entire class of genetically-defined neurodegenerative diseases that are today undruggable — and the team that solves delivery owns the category.*

The single hardest problem in central-nervous-system drug development is not knowing *which* gene to silence — RNAi has solved gene silencing elsewhere in the body — it is getting the drug **across the blood–brain barrier**. Aerska is a focused bet on the delivery problem, led by people who have already built RNAi companies and pioneered brain-delivery chemistry. Solve delivery once and it is a platform, not a drug: the same shuttle carries many payloads into many diseases.

## 2. What they do

RNA-interference (RNAi) drugs switch off disease-causing genes. They work well in the liver but cannot reach the brain when injected into the body, because the blood–brain barrier blocks them. Aerska attaches the RNAi payload to an **antibody-oligonucleotide conjugate ("brain shuttle")** that binds a receptor on the barrier's cells and is ferried across — the goal being **intravenous or subcutaneous dosing** (not risky direct-to-brain injection) that achieves deep, uniform, durable gene knockdown. The company is initially aiming at **genetically driven forms of Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease**.

**Stage:** pre-clinical / platform. No named clinical candidate or IND timeline has been disclosed — this is an early, science-stage bet.

## 3. Market

- **Alzheimer's:** tens of millions of patients worldwide; a market in the tens of billions of dollars. The recently approved anti-amyloid antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) have modest efficacy and meaningful safety burdens (ARIA), leaving wide room for a mechanistically different, upstream approach.
- **Parkinson's:** millions of patients and **no approved disease-modifying therapy** — a large, open unmet need.
- **Who pays:** ultimately large pharma. CNS delivery is strategically prized, evidenced by GSK's ~$2.5B (April 2025) deal for blood-brain-barrier technology. A validated shuttle is an acquisition or major-partnership target well before a product reaches patients.

## 4. Team

The reason to underwrite an otherwise very early company:

- **Jack O'Meara (CEO, co-founder)** — previously founded and led Ochre Bio (RNAi for liver disease; raised $100M+ with billion-dollar-scale pharma milestones). He has built an RNAi company before.
- **Stuart Milstein (co-founder)** — RNAi pioneer who initiated Alnylam's brain-delivery program and helped define industry-standard siRNA chemistry. This is delivery expertise at the source.
- **Mike Perkinton (Head of Research)** — former head of discovery, AstraZeneca Neuroscience.

Backed at Series A by **EQT Life Sciences (LSP Dementia Fund)** and **age1**, with board additions including a prominent Alzheimer's neurologist (Philip Scheltens). This is a specialist syndicate, not tourists.

## 6. Risks (pre-mortem)

*The specific ways this loses all our money, ranked:*

1. **Delivery doesn't work at therapeutic doses (fatal, most likely).** Brain-shuttle receptor transport, uniform deep-brain distribution, and durable knockdown are all unproven for this platform. If the shuttle can't carry a therapeutic dose across the barrier, there is no company. This is the whole bet.
2. **Beaten to the category.** Denali (Transport Vehicle), Roche/Genentech (Brain Shuttle), and Ionis/Alnylam are well-capitalised and ahead on CNS delivery. Aerska must be differentiated *and* fast.
3. **Undercapitalised for CNS.** $60M is modest for neurodegeneration. Reaching the clinic will require a large Series B or a pharma partnership; a frozen funding market could strand a pre-clinical company.
4. **Key-person risk.** The entire thesis rests on a small elite team; losing Milstein or O'Meara would materially impair it.

## 7. The bet & the plan

**Conviction: high on team and problem, deliberately early on evidence.** This is a classic power-law platform bet: most likely returns nothing, but the tail — owning even a small slice of the company that solves CNS delivery — is enormous and could contribute to returning the fund via an early pharma acquisition.

**What I do in the 18 months after wiring:**
- **Underwrite the one thing that matters:** define, with the company, the specific pre-clinical delivery readout (dose, distribution, durability, species) that would justify a Series B double-down — and treat everything else as noise until then.
- **Reserve aggressively (1.5x)** and make maintaining/growing ownership at Series B the priority use of those reserves, conditional on that readout.
- **Monitor the competitive clock:** track Denali/Roche/Ionis CNS-delivery milestones quarterly; Aerska's value is relative to the field's progress, not absolute.
- **Introduce pharma BD early** — CNS-delivery-hungry acquirers (the GSK deal is the template) should know this asset before it needs them.

**Kill criteria:** if the first delivery readout shows sub-therapeutic brain exposure or non-durable knockdown with no credible chemistry fix, we do **not** follow on, and we write the position down.
